Keyflex AI:
Perspective & Potential Support

For Camille Yan & JR Pagdanganan

What I Heard

First, you can build almost anything with AI now, so the hard problem is deciding what to build next, and that call has to come from customer signal rather than founder instinct.

Second, one developer carries the entire product, so every new design partner competes for the same hours, and you need a way to grow demand without losing control of what you can ship.

Third, founder-led attention on your priority customers is the right call, since high NPS and design-partner trust depend on it, but that same hands-on approach won't stretch across 76 warm leads and 300 attendees, and the rest of the list needs a parallel track.

Keyflex AI is its own first customer. You built it inside Keyflex Ventures, a midterm-rental operation that crossed $2M in revenue and peaked near 26 units, because you ran the workflow yourself and knew the tooling didn't exist. Most vertical-AI founders guess at the operator's day. You lived it.

How I Can Help

My work at Bastille is fractional and sprint-based. I come in for defined phases, deliver specific things, and leave the team stronger. The work falls into three areas.

  • Operating Plan & Scope Discipline
    • Map the milestones from today to a defined target: recurring billing from Rebecca, the first cohort signed, and the point where the product pays for its own development.
    • Build the iteration-scoping framework: define the signals that trigger a build decision, so the roadmap moves on customer evidence, not on whatever is possible that week.
    • Turn the six-month roadmap into a sprint plan divided into two-week sprints, so you and JR see what ships when and protect focus against scope creep.
    • Translate cohort feedback into a prioritized feature queue, weighting the 20-to-40-unit operators who share the same problems and setting aside the outliers that pollute the signal.
  • Pipeline Activation
    • Tranche the 76 warm leads and 300 attendees into priority groups, with tier one routed to Camille for founder-led sales and the rest to a scaled process.
    • Run stakeholder interviews at scale: 15-minute calls with tier-two and tier-three operators that gather workflow detail and close with a soft introduction to what you shipped that week.
    • Define design-partner intake: a fixed scope for what a design partner gets, so each one stays inside what JR can deliver.
    • Stand up a private update channel for operators who complete an interview, where they watch their feedback turn into shipped features and stay warm until the product is ready for them.
    • Set up outreach as a campaign rather than one-off sends: sourcing, sequencing, and tooling, built in the stack you already pay for or in Claude Code.
  • Fundraise Readiness

    Your call, when you want it.

    • Comp your milestones to the numbers a raise requires: the ARR, customer count, and retention the firms funding vertical AI fund against.
    • Open direct conversations with those investors, so your targets reflect what they fund.
    • Build the narrative from the operator-built origin and cohort traction, framed for the people who write the check.
    • Make the introductions. My network includes a real part of the vertical-AI investor base.

How I Work

I work as an embedded operator, inside the company on the actual work. Engagements run as monthly retainers built around sprints, with milestones set at the start and reviewed at the end. We start with one month. At the close we check what we set out to hit, what we hit, and why, then decide whether to continue. 50% upfront, work begins on receipt. Month one produces a milestone map from June to your target, a two-week sprint plan against the six-month roadmap, the iteration-scoping framework, a tranched outreach list with a stakeholder-interview process, and the structure for your design-partner nurture channel.
