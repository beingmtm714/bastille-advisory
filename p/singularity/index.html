Singularity:
Ecosystem Activation & Builder Pipeline

For Ahyaan Sayed, Naman, and the Singularity team

What I Heard

First, Singularity spent months wrapping the chain in application layers that obscured what it is: a horizontally scalable L1 with multi-language smart contract support and native cross-ecosystem composability.

Second, the team has committed to a two-track strategy — bring in a first tranche of early-stage builders who believe in the vision while developing an in-house application — but the ecosystem activation infrastructure to source, qualify, and onboard those builders does not exist yet.

Third, Bishar and his BD team cover social, content, brand, and protocol intros. The missing function is a systematic pipeline that sources early-stage builders and emerging protocols, qualifies them against Singularity's technical differentiation, and moves them from first contact to testnet deployment. This first tranche is not general-purpose developer acquisition. These builders are Singularity's core evangelists. They need to be selected for both technical fit and amplification potential, then equipped with the tools and context to credibly advocate for the ecosystem when the in-house app launches and programmatic scaling begins.

What I Found

  • Developer acquisition is the defining L1 competition. Ethereum added 16,181 new developers in the first nine months of 2025; Solana added 11,534. Ecosystems below the top three trail under 7,000. Electric Capital's annual report is the benchmark investors and partners use to assess ecosystem health.
  • AI agent infrastructure creates a timing window. The agentic AI market reached $7.3B in 2025, projected at $9–11B in 2026, growing 40–46% year over year. Google, AWS, and Visa shipped blockchain-based machine-to-machine transaction protocols in late 2025. Autonomous agents need rails built for negotiation and execution between machines. Singularity's positioning as purpose-built agent infrastructure targets the fastest-growing demand category in crypto.
  • The highest-value builder targets are protocols already deployed on 3+ chains. Multi-chain DeFi protocols, gaming studios, and agent infrastructure companies maintain separate deployments across Ethereum, Solana, Base, Avalanche, and others. Each deployment requires separate audits, governance, and engineering maintenance. Singularity's native composability pitch — deploy once, reach users across all chains — converts the multi-chain operational tax into the acquisition wedge. Builders already paying that tax are the highest-probability converts.
  • Solana's Superteam model proves the ecosystem sourcing playbook. Superteam distributed over $1.7M in community GDP through microgrants, bounties, and direct builder engagement. The model combines active sourcing with a clear onboarding path. Singularity needs a version of this scaled to its stage.
  • Established L1s are investing aggressively in builder incentives. Avalanche launched a $40M Retro9000 grant program in Q1 2026. Base onboarded 1,600 developers in its first year. Singularity does not need to match these dollar figures, but it needs a systematic pipeline that competes for the same builders' attention.
  • The headwind is the cold-start problem. A new L1 without testnet deployments faces a credibility gap with builders who prefer ecosystems with visible traction. The first 10–20 active builders on the testnet create the social proof that unlocks the next 100. The acquisition strategy — deploy alongside your existing chain, then consolidate to Singularity as composability proves itself — lowers the switching cost to near zero, but the pipeline has to generate those first deployments fast. These early builders carry a second job: they are the distribution channel for Singularity's narrative before a marketing budget exists. Selecting them for reach matters as much as selecting them for technical alignment.
